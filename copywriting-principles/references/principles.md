# The 22 Copywriting Principles

## 1. Write with your eraser
Cut every word you can. Shorter almost always wins.
"Trello lets you work more collaboratively and get more done" becomes "Work collaboratively. Get more done."

## 2. No one cares what you can do
People care what it does for them.
"The world's first portable digital media player" becomes "1,000 songs in your pocket."

## 3. Avoid the passive voice
Passive is indirect and awkward.
"It's being loved by me" becomes "I'm lovin' it."

## 4. Speak with conviction
Say "it's how," not "we help." Say "replaces," not "alternative."
"We help you make a podcast" becomes "It's how you make a podcast."

## 5. Avoid landing page words
Unlock, unleash, enhance, exceed, empower, supercharge. Real people don't talk like this.

## 6. Find the tension
Pleasant gets forgotten. Conflict creates interest.
"Insurance for the digital revolution" becomes "Forget everything you know about insurance."

## 7. Write how you talk
Casual, colloquial, full of pronouns. Read it out loud, if it sounds stiff, rewrite it.

## 8. Don't exaggerate
An honest line feels warmer than a grand one.
"The new Volkswagen. More space. More comfort." becomes "If you run out of gas, it's easy to push."

## 9. Avoid contained titles
Write something that pulls the reader down the page instead of closing the thought.
"Social media intelligence for your startup" becomes "Do you know the value of your Facebook content?"

## 10. Call to value, not call to action
Buttons that promise an outcome beat buttons that describe an action.
"Sign up now" becomes "Create your website." "Get started" becomes "Hire top designers."

## 11. Don't kill your personality
The best brands sound like a real person, not a press release.

## 12. More periods, fewer commas
Short sentences read easier. Long comma chained sentences wear the reader out.

## 13. Use value based messaging
Sell the outcome, not the feature.
"Introducing the new and improved Colgate" becomes "Smile like you've never smiled before."

## 14. Kill adverbs
They're vague and they try too hard.
"Effortlessly create email lists from LinkedIn" becomes "Create email lists from LinkedIn."

## 15. Think slippery slide
Every line should pull the reader to the next line, all the way to the CTA.

## 16. Get specific
Exact numbers beat vague claims, because they can't be argued with.
"Save more on your AWS bill" becomes "Save an average of 33.7% on your AWS bill."

## 17. Fence sitters don't buy
Take a real stance instead of hedging toward the middle.

## 18. Your first line is crucial
If the first line doesn't earn the second line, nothing after it matters.
"We focus on nearly every muscle in our body, yet neglect the one area we value most, our face" becomes "Your face has 43 muscles."

## 19. Call out the customer you serve
Naming a specific audience gets more attention than trying to speak to everyone.
"A creative hub for anyone and everyone" becomes "A creative hub for 150,000 authors."

## 20. You're on a speed date
Most people give a page six seconds. If the product can't be made interesting that fast, sell the benefit instead.
"All-in-one social automation" becomes "Turn followers into customers."

## 21. Copywriting is selling
The goal is action, not being clever.
"Request a demo" becomes "See Drift on your site."

## 22. Reason and proof on every ask
Every time copy asks the reader to do something, two psychological levers make that ask land harder.

**The reason effect.** People comply with a request more often when a reason is attached to it, even a small or fairly obvious one. A request that just states the ask reads as an order. A request that states the ask plus a reason reads as reasonable.
Example: "Sign up now" becomes "Sign up now because early access closes Friday."

**Social proof.** People look to what others are already doing to decide what they should do. An ask that shows other people already complying lowers resistance to comply.
Example: "Book a call" becomes "Join the 400+ teams who've already booked their setup call."

When auditing copy, check every CTA and every direct ask for both of these. If a reason is missing, add one, even a simple one works. If social proof is missing and real proof exists (client counts, results, reviews), work it into the line closest to the ask.
